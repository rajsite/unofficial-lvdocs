Classifying Filters by Impulse Response

The impulse response of a filter determines whether the filter is an FIR or IIR filter. The output of an FIR filter depends only on the current and past input values. The output of an IIR filter depends on the current and past input values and the current and past output values.

The operation of a cash register can serve as an example to illustrate the difference between FIR and IIR filter operations. For this example, the following conditions are true:

  • x[k] is the cost of the current item entered into the cash register.
  • x[k - 1] is the price of the past item entered into the cash register.
  • 1 ≤ k ≤ N
  • N is the total number of items entered into the cash register.

The following statements describe the operation of the cash register:

  • The cash register adds the cost of each item to produce the running total y[k].
  • The following equation computes y[k] up to the kth item.

    y[k] = x[k] + x[k - 1] + x[k - 2] + x[k - 3] + … + x[1]

    Therefore, the total for N items is y[N].

  • y[k] equals the total up to the kth item.

    y[k - 1] equals the total up to the (k - 1) item.

    Therefore, the previous equation can be rewritten as the following equation.

    y[k] = y[k - 1] + x[k]

  • Add a tax of 8.25% and rewrite the previous two equations as the following equations.

    y[k] = 1.0825x[k] + 1.0825x[k - 1] + 1.0825x[k - 2] + 1.0825x[k - 3] + … + 1.0825x[1]

    y[k] = y[k - 1] + 1.0825x[k]

The two equations after adding the tax identically describe the behavior of the cash register. However, the first equation describes the behavior of the cash register only in terms of the input, while the second equation describes the behavior in terms of both the input and the output. The first equation represents a non-recursive, or FIR, operation. The second equation represents a recursive, or IIR, operation.

Equations that describe the operation of a filter and have the same form as the previous four equations are difference equations.

FIR filters are the simplest filters to design. If a single impulse is present at the input of an FIR filter and all subsequent inputs are zero, the output of an FIR filter becomes zero after a finite time. Therefore, FIR filters are finite. The time required for the filter output to reach zero equals the number of filter coefficients.

Because IIR filters operate on current and past input values and current and past output values, the impulse response of an IIR filter never reaches zero and is an infinite response.

In the following equation,

y[k] = 1.0825x[k] + 1.0825x[k - 1] + 1.0825x[k - 2] + 1.0825x[k - 3] + … + 1.0825x[1]

the multiplying constant for each term is 1.0825.

In the following equation,

y[k] = y[k - 1] + 1.0825x[k]

the multiplying constants are 1 for y[k-1] and 1.0825 for x[k].

The multiplying constants are the coefficients of the filter. For an IIR filter, the coefficients multiplying the inputs are the forward coefficients. The coefficients multiplying the outputs are the reverse coefficients.
